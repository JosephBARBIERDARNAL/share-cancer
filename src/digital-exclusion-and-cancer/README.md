# Digital Exclusion and Missed Cancer Screening Among Older Adults in Europe

## Executive summary

Your preliminary finding is credible, policy-relevant, and plausibly novel. The broader literature strongly supports the idea that digital technologies now shape access to preventive care, but it is much thinner on whether **digital exclusion itself** remains associated with missed cancer screening **after** adjusting for socioeconomic status, health literacy, health-care contact, disability, and country context. What the higher-confidence literature does show is that digital access and digital skills are socially patterned among older adults; that use of eHealth tools, telemedicine, portals, and online booking is lower among older, less educated, poorer, rurally located, and digitally less skilled groups; and that cancer screening uptake in Europe remains stratified by education, income, geography, and screening-program organization. citeturn27view1turn29view0turn30view0turn30view2turn40view0turn41search1turn41search3turn26view1

The screening-specific digital literature is most developed for **breast screening** rather than colorectal screening. Studies of online mammography self-scheduling, portal-based communication, and breast-screening digital pathways consistently suggest that digital channels can affect attendance and that these channels are used least by groups already at risk of exclusion. For colorectal screening, the literature more often studies digital interventions or general health literacy than digital exclusion per se. citeturn13view6turn16search1turn14search5turn15view4turn17search1turn20search7

That asymmetry matters for interpreting your results. A stronger adjusted association for **missed mammography** than for **missed colorectal screening** is consistent with the existing evidence because mammography more often requires in-person imaging appointments, appointment management, and increasingly digital communication; by contrast, colorectal screening is more heterogeneous, often involving home-based stool tests as well as endoscopy, so the “digital friction” may be more diluted. European program organization also differs sharply across countries and cancer sites, with organized call-recall systems generally more equitable than opportunistic models. citeturn13view6turn16search1turn38view2turn38view0turn23view1turn24view1turn26view4

The clearest gap your study can fill is this: in the literature retrieved for this review, I did **not** identify a peer-reviewed study that directly tests whether digital exclusion, measured as lack of recent internet use and/or poor computer skills, is independently associated with missed **breast and colorectal screening simultaneously** in **SHARE** after adjustment for **SES, health literacy, health-care utilization, ADL limitations, BMI, and country fixed effects**. Existing SHARE-based work has examined internet use among older Europeans, remote health-care use, and preventive-care inequalities, but not that full combination. This makes your design meaningfully contributory. citeturn30view0turn29view0turn30view2turn40view0

## Conceptual framework

In your project, **digital exclusion** is broader than simple lack of broadband and narrower than the full concept of health literacy. The literature distinguishes several related constructs. **Internet access** refers to infrastructure and device/connectivity availability. **Internet use** refers to actual recent online engagement. **Computer skills** refer to operational ability to use devices and interfaces. **Digital literacy** or **digital health literacy** refers to the broader capacity to use digital tools and services. **eHealth literacy**, in the classic formulation, is the ability to seek, find, understand, appraise, and apply health information from electronic sources. **Health literacy** is broader still: the ability to obtain, understand, evaluate, and use health information in any format, not only digital. citeturn12search9turn12search7turn12search2

That distinction is important because your exposure combines two layers of the digital pathway: **behavioral nonuse** and **skill deficit**. It therefore sits upstream of digital health-system navigation and eHealth literacy, and only partly overlaps with education or general health literacy. In other words, someone can have adequate formal education or even reasonable general health literacy yet still be digitally excluded if they do not go online or cannot manage computer-based tasks. The recent literature increasingly treats this as a distinct social determinant of access to care rather than a mere proxy for poverty or schooling. citeturn12search2turn27view1

A useful causal diagram for your paper is:

**Socioeconomic position, ageing, disability, rurality, migration background, and country context**  
→ **internet access / recent use / computer skills**  
→ **eHealth literacy, confidence, trust, and ability to navigate digital systems**  
→ **exposure to screening information, ability to receive reminders, portal use, online booking/rescheduling, communication with providers**  
→ **screening completion or missed screening**. citeturn27view1turn29view0turn30view2turn27view0

On this framework, your covariate set is unusually strong. Education and income absorb part of the structural digital divide. Health literacy absorbs some non-digital comprehension. GP and specialist contacts absorb opportunities for clinician recommendation. ADL limitation and BMI absorb some physical barriers and health need. Country fixed effects absorb stable national differences in program organization and digital infrastructure. If digital exclusion remains associated after all that, the most plausible interpretation is not “pure causality,” but rather that digital exclusion captures a **residual access-and-navigation barrier** that is not fully reducible to SES, general literacy, or health-care contact alone. citeturn27view1turn26view3turn13view6

## Structured literature review

**Digital exclusion and preventive health care**

The strongest general evidence now comes from studies of eHealth use and digital access in Europe. A 2026 systematic review of 18 observational studies from European public health-care systems found persistent social gradients in the use of online appointment booking, teleconsultations, EHR access, and portals: older adults, people with lower education or socioeconomic position, ethnic minorities, people with poorer health, and those with limited digital skills were less likely to use these tools. Importantly, the review framed these inequalities as a feature of routine health-system digitalization, not just voluntary “extra” app use. citeturn27view1

Earlier European work points in the same direction. Merkel and Hess, using Special Eurobarometer 460, showed that the probability of using internet-based health and care services in Europe was higher among younger, better educated, higher social-class, and urban respondents, and also varied with country-level adult training activity. Their conclusion is directly relevant to your study: the advantages of digital health services depend strongly on socioeconomic background and broader learning context. citeturn29view0

Among older Europeans specifically, the basic precursor is unequal internet use itself. König and colleagues, using SHARE data from 17 countries and more than 61,000 adults aged 50 and older, documented a large age-graded and socially patterned divide in internet use. That matters because no one can benefit from digital invitation systems, information campaigns, or portal-based navigation if they are not online in the first place. citeturn30view0

The COVID period accelerated the relevance of these mechanisms. In the SHARE Corona Survey, Smolić and colleagues found that adults aged 80 and older were the least likely to use remote care, while better educated people, urban residents, those without financial strain, and **active internet users** were more likely to use remote consultations. In Ireland, Mao and colleagues showed longitudinally in TILDA that prior internet access predicted telemedicine use in 2020; older adults with internet access in 2018 had roughly 1.6 times the odds of using telemedicine in 2020, and daily internet users also had higher odds, even with extensive adjustment. These studies do not examine cancer screening, but they are strong evidence that older adults’ preventive and access-related care increasingly depends on digital capability. citeturn30view2turn33view2turn33view3

Evidence outside Europe points in the same direction for routine prevention. In the HRS, Nam and colleagues found that internet use among older couples was associated with a higher likelihood of some preventive behaviors, including cholesterol testing, flu shots, and prostate testing, net of demographic, health, and insurance factors. The exact services differ from your outcomes, but the mechanism is highly relevant: internet use is linked to better uptake of routine prevention in later life. citeturn30view3

Finally, the policy environment now makes digital exclusion more consequential than it was a decade ago. The OECD reported in 2026 that even where electronic records and digital systems are widespread, patient awareness and meaningful engagement remain limited; on average only 18% of patients knew they could access their own records online through primary care. In other words, infrastructure alone does not eliminate access barriers. As systems digitalize, the gap between “available” and “usable” services becomes a real equity issue. citeturn27view0

**Digital exclusion and cancer screening**

The cancer-screening-specific literature is real but uneven. For **mammography**, the evidence is strongest. Balthazar and colleagues examined online self-scheduling of screening mammography at a large academic center and found that online self-scheduling was extremely uncommon overall, but was more likely among younger women, English speakers, White non-Hispanic women, those with commercial insurance, and those living in areas with more computers and internet access. Area median household income was not significant after adjustment, suggesting that digital access is not simply a proxy for income in these models. This is one of the clearest examples of a digital pathway shaping actual screening logistics. citeturn13view6

Alabdullatif and colleagues, using repeated U.S. national survey data, similarly found that IT-based health-care communication was associated with mammography utilization across 2011–2018. Although this is not an older-adult-only European study, it directly supports a core mechanism in your paper: women who are more connected to digital communication channels are more likely to receive preventive screening. citeturn14search5

There is also evidence that when organized screening programs add digital appointment tools, uptake can change. Chiereghin and colleagues reported that, in an organized breast-screening program in Italy, use of an online appointment portal had a positive effect on attendance by reducing no-shows; portal use also increased during the COVID period. This matters because it shows that digital interfaces can influence actual completion, not just information exposure. citeturn16search1

The review literature on breast cancer and the digital divide reaches the same broad conclusion. Bayard and colleagues concluded that telehealth and digital infrastructure can improve access to screening and care, but unequal broadband access and unequal telehealth uptake risk magnifying breast cancer disparities in vulnerable populations. That review is not confined to screening attendance, but it places your question squarely inside a larger oncology equity debate. citeturn15view4

For **colorectal screening**, direct evidence on digital exclusion as a barrier is thinner. Older foundational work showed that eHealth literacy was associated with colorectal cancer knowledge and screening practice, but this literature is much more limited than the mammography literature. More recent reviews suggest that digital tools may help reach vulnerable invitees and improve informed decision-making, yet the evidence base remains heterogeneous and focused more on interventions than on digital exclusion as an independently measured exposure. citeturn17search1turn20search7

A particularly important comparison is with the health-literacy literature. Baccolini and colleagues’ 2022 systematic review and meta-analysis found that adequate health literacy was associated with higher participation in breast, cervical, and colorectal screening even after adjustment: pooled adjusted odds ratios were 1.73 for breast screening and 1.25 for colorectal screening. That tells us two things. First, informational and navigation capacities do matter independently of classic demographics. Second, the association tends to be **stronger for breast screening than for colorectal screening**, which is very much in line with your pattern. citeturn18search5

But this is not completely uniform across systems. In Denmark’s highly organized screening context, more recent studies found no clear association between health literacy and breast-screening attendance, no association with colorectal screening uptake, and no overall significant association across the three national screening programs. Those studies imply that highly structured organized systems may compress literacy-related inequalities. That makes your result more interesting, not less: if digital exclusion still matters after health literacy adjustment in a cross-national European older sample, it may be picking up a distinct barrier that organized systems do not fully neutralize. citeturn42search1turn42search2turn42search0

**Cancer screening inequalities among older adults in Europe**

The broader European literature is unequivocal that mammography and colorectal screening are unequally distributed. Carrieri and Wuebker, using SHARE, found high inequalities in mammography use in virtually every country they studied, largely driven by income, and reported that mammography inequalities were higher where national breast screening programs were absent or weakly developed. They explicitly linked preventive-care inequality to health-system organization, not just to individual demand. citeturn40view0

Willems and Bracke later showed that the education gradient in cancer screening participation is a consistent phenomenon across Europe. Bozhar and colleagues, in a 2022 cross-sectional European study, concluded that there is considerable inequality in the use of cancer testing associated with household income, education, employment status, and marital status. These are precisely the inequalities your models already control for, which sharpens the interpretation of the residual digital effect. citeturn41search1turn41search3

The latest OECD and European Cancer Inequalities Registry reports add detail. In EU+2 countries, women with low education were less likely to have mammography in 19 countries; for colorectal screening, people with lower education had lower uptake than those with higher education, and rural residents had significantly lower odds of both breast and colon screening even after full adjustment for core individual characteristics. Migration background also mattered for breast screening before SES adjustment, though part of that gradient was explained by income and education. citeturn26view0turn26view1

Country differences are very large. The OECD reported that in the most recent comparable data, mammography coverage among eligible women ranged from 83% in Denmark to 9% in Romania, and that participation was below 50% in 11 EU+2 countries for breast screening and in 21 countries for colorectal screening. Only Austria, Denmark, Finland, the Netherlands, and Slovenia exceeded 50% for all three major screening programs. The JRC colorectal factsheet likewise reported that in 2019, 49% of Europeans aged 50–74 had never attended colorectal screening, with country values ranging from 17% to 94%. citeturn26view4turn23view1

Program organization is central to understanding these inequalities. Organized, population-based programs with registries and call-recall systems tend to be more equitable than opportunistic systems because they reduce the amount of initiative and health-system navigation required from the individual. Recent evidence supports this for both breast and colorectal screening. Guthmuller and colleagues estimated that organized breast screening programs in Europe increased mammography uptake by about 25 percentage points, with mortality reductions emerging later. In Sweden, Pedrós Barnils and Gustafsson found that organized colorectal screening regions displayed much more limited inequities than opportunistic regions, where migration, gender, and income gradients were larger. Broader European sources make the same point: organized programs are more equitable because they actively reach those otherwise most likely to be missed. citeturn38view0turn38view2turn24view1

Social isolation is less well studied than SES, education, or rurality in the screening literature, but the available evidence still fits your framework. Recent work on bowel screening suggests that weaker social connections or lower social support are associated with lower uptake, and separate European ageing research indicates that internet use among older adults is linked to lower social isolation. Taken together, these studies suggest that digital exclusion may matter partly because it weakens access to social reinforcement, informal help, and household-level navigation support. citeturn43search7turn43search16turn35search0

**SHARE-specific and comparable ageing-dataset evidence**

SHARE is especially well suited to your question because it covers older adults across European systems and contains both prevention and digital-use variables. The SHARE questionnaire includes items on mammography and colon cancer screening over the prior two years, and wave 8 documentation reported that only 32% of respondents aged 50–74 had a colon cancer screening test in the prior two years, with major cross-national variation. That provides direct descriptive context for your wave 9 analyses. citeturn23view3

The SHARE-based literature already contains two strands that your paper bridges. One strand studies digital use among older Europeans. König and colleagues showed strong cross-national and socioeconomic gradients in internet use; Smolić and colleagues showed that active internet users were more likely to use remote care during COVID; and Merkel and Hess showed that use of internet-based health and care services depends on education, social class, geography, and country learning environment. The other strand studies preventive care and screening inequalities. Carrieri and Wuebker used SHARE to document EU-wide pro-rich mammography inequalities and the importance of national screening design. Your paper connects those two strands directly. citeturn30view0turn30view2turn29view0turn40view0

Comparable ageing datasets tell a similar story. In HRS, internet use is associated with several preventive behaviors in later life. In TILDA, prior internet access predicts later telemedicine adoption. These studies are not cancer-screening analyses in Europe, but they strengthen the plausibility of a general pathway from digital engagement to preventive-care uptake among older adults. citeturn30view3turn33view2

In the literature retrieved for this review, I did not locate a peer-reviewed study directly examining **digital exclusion and missed breast/colorectal screening in SHARE**. The SHARE papers I found addressed internet use, remote health-care use, or preventive-care inequality separately. That appears to be the main niche your study can fill. citeturn30view0turn29view0turn30view2turn40view0

**Mechanisms linking digital exclusion to missed screening**

The most plausible mechanisms are layered rather than singular. First, digital exclusion can impair **access to online booking and rescheduling**, especially for mammography, where facility-based appointments are common and portal studies show clear selection into digital scheduling. Second, it can reduce **exposure to health information**, reminders, and targeted campaigns, especially as systems increasingly rely on portals, websites, text reminders, and digitally mediated information flow. Third, it can hinder **health-system navigation**, especially when preventive pathways require interpreting a mailed invitation, verifying eligibility, arranging an appointment, or following up after an abnormal result. citeturn13view6turn16search1turn27view1turn27view0

Fourth, digital exclusion is linked to lower **eHealth literacy** and lower confidence using health information online. Fifth, it may correspond to weaker **patient activation** and lower confidence in dealing with bureaucratic or institutional systems, although that part of the pathway is still more often inferred than directly measured in screening studies. Sixth, digital exclusion can weaken **provider communication**, because many systems now route reminders, scheduling opportunities, lab access, and education through digital tools. OECD and European evidence suggests that digital service availability does not translate automatically into actual patient use, particularly for older adults and vulnerable groups. citeturn12search9turn12search2turn27view1turn27view0

Seventh, the relationship likely interacts with **socioeconomic disadvantage** but is not reducible to it. Balthazar’s mammography scheduling study is instructive here: neighborhood computer/internet access mattered, while income was not independently significant. More generally, the European eHealth inequalities literature shows strong but not perfectly overlapping gradients by age, education, SES, ethnicity, and digital skills. That is why adjusting for education and income does not necessarily erase a digital association. citeturn13view6turn27view1

Eighth, the **COVID and post-COVID digitalization era** likely amplified these mechanisms. Older adults who were already digitally engaged were more able to use remote care during the pandemic, and systems accelerated use of teleconsultations, portals, and online interfaces. For screening specifically, COVID also pushed some programs toward more flexible digital communication and portal use. Your wave 9 timing therefore makes substantive sense: you are studying screening behavior after a major digital shock to European health systems. citeturn30view2turn33view2turn16search1turn27view1

## Comparison with the SHARE Wave 9 results

Your results fit the literature in four important ways.

First, the association attenuates with adjustment but does not disappear. That is exactly what one would expect if digital exclusion partly overlaps with age and socioeconomic disadvantage but also captures something additional about navigation, communication, and system usability. The broader European digital-health literature shows that age, education, and SES all matter, yet digital skills and digital engagement retain independent importance within health-system access. citeturn27view1turn29view0turn33view2

Second, the **magnitude** of your adjusted effects is plausible. Your fully adjusted OR of **1.31** for missed colon cancer screening is very close to the pooled adjusted association reported in the health-literacy meta-analysis for colorectal screening participation (**aOR 1.25** for adequate versus inadequate health literacy, reversed in direction conceptually but similar in scale). Your fully adjusted OR of **1.72** for missed mammography is strikingly close to the pooled adjusted association for breast-screening participation (**aOR 1.73**). That does not imply the constructs are equivalent, but it does suggest that a digital-exclusion coefficient of this size is substantively believable, not an implausible artifact. citeturn18search5

Third, your result is stronger than much of the purely SES-based literature in one crucial respect: you retain association **after** adjusting for health-care contacts and health literacy. In many prior European screening inequality studies, SES gradients are the central focus; in many digital studies, the outcome is eHealth use rather than actual screening. Your model therefore moves the literature forward by asking whether digital exclusion matters once both material position and service exposure are held constant. Based on the literature retrieved for this review, that combination is still uncommon. citeturn40view0turn41search1turn41search3turn27view1

Fourth, the stronger association for **mammography** than for **colorectal screening** is highly interpretable.

A likely explanation is **screening pathway specificity**. In SHARE, the colon cancer item groups together stool blood tests, sigmoidoscopy, and colonoscopy within the prior two years. That is a heterogeneous outcome: a mailed or pharmacy-distributed stool test may require little digital navigation, whereas colonoscopy may come through an opportunistic physician pathway. Mammography, by contrast, is a more standardized facility-based encounter that usually requires appointment attendance and often rescheduling. If digital exclusion creates friction at the booking, reminder, or logistics stage, it should show up more clearly for mammography. citeturn23view3turn13view6turn16search1

Another explanation is **gendered preventive-care pathways**. Women are often more embedded in screening systems and preventive-care contacts than men, which can make the remaining barriers more visible at the point of appointment management and follow-through. At the same time, mammography programs are generally more established than colorectal programs in many European countries, so invitation status may be clearer while digital barriers to actual completion remain. This could generate a larger residual digital coefficient for mammography, not because breast screening is less organized, but because digital exclusion operates at a later stage in a better-developed pathway. citeturn38view0turn26view4turn26view3

A third explanation is **organized versus opportunistic mix**. Organized programs tend to reduce inequality, but they do not eliminate it. The breast literature shows that organized programs substantially raise uptake overall, while CRC evidence from Sweden suggests organized systems compress inequity relative to opportunistic regions. Yet organized systems still require response to invitations, comprehension of instructions, and often appointment management. A woman who is digitally excluded may therefore still miss mammography even in an organized system. By contrast, the colorectal outcome may contain more opportunistic and non-digital pathways, which can dilute a single cross-national digital-exclusion coefficient. citeturn38view0turn38view2turn24view1turn23view1

A fourth explanation is **post-COVID digitalization**. The pandemic accelerated remote communication, portal use, and digital expectations. Breast-screening services may have expanded digital scheduling and communication faster or more visibly than some colorectal pathways, especially FIT-based strategies that can still rely on mail and laboratory workflow. If so, wave 9 would naturally reveal a stronger mammography association. The literature supports this as a plausible interpretation, although it has not been tested directly enough at the cross-national European older-adult level. citeturn30view2turn33view2turn16search1turn27view1

## Limitations, gaps, and paper development

Your study should be framed as evidence of **association**, not causation. The design is observational and likely cross-sectional with respect to much of the screening and digital-use information, so reverse causation cannot be ruled out completely. People who are more prevention-oriented may also be more likely to stay digitally engaged; conversely, poor health, cognitive decline, depression, or frailty may both reduce digital participation and reduce screening uptake. Even though you adjust for ADL limitations, BMI, and health-care contact, some residual confounding by cognition, sensory impairment, invitation receipt, prior negative experiences, or household support may remain. citeturn27view1turn12search2turn30view2

Measurement also matters. Your digital-exclusion measure is a strength because it combines skills and recent use, but it still does not identify the exact mechanism: lack of device access, lack of confidence, lack of portal credentials, distrust, fear of scams, or inability to navigate booking sites. Likewise, the SHARE colorectal outcome combines multiple tests with different intervals, delivery channels, and patient burdens. That is analytically practical, but it likely attenuates mechanism-specific inference. citeturn23view3turn12search2turn27view1

Cross-national heterogeneity is another limitation and a substantive opportunity. Country fixed effects absorb average national differences, but they cannot fully capture within-country program heterogeneity, regional implementation differences, local portal design, invitation practices, or specific degrees of post-COVID digitalization. This matters especially for colorectal screening, where target ages, test modalities, and implementation dates vary substantially across Europe. citeturn23view1turn26view4

At the same time, these are exactly the gaps that make your paper valuable. The literature retrieved here still under-studies:

Digital exclusion **as a distinct construct** rather than a side effect of education or income;  
older adults in **cross-national Europe** rather than single-country convenience samples;  
actual **screening behavior** rather than portal enrollment or eHealth use;  
simultaneous comparison of **breast and colorectal** screening in the same design;  
models that adjust not only SES but also **health literacy and health-care contact**;  
the **post-COVID** period, when digitalization became more central to routine care;  
and, based on this search, direct analysis of **digital exclusion and screening in SHARE**. citeturn27view1turn29view0turn30view2turn40view0turn20search7

**Suggested hypotheses**

- **H1:** Digital exclusion is associated with higher odds of missed colorectal and breast cancer screening among screening-eligible older adults in Europe, independent of socioeconomic status, health literacy, health-care contact, and country fixed effects.
- **H2:** The association between digital exclusion and missed screening is stronger for mammography than for colorectal screening.
- **H3:** Adjustment for education and income attenuates but does not eliminate the association between digital exclusion and missed screening.
- **H4:** Adjustment for GP and specialist contact attenuates but does not eliminate the association, implying that digital exclusion is not merely a proxy for lower provider exposure.
- **H5:** The association is stronger in contexts where screening requires more active patient navigation, such as appointment-based imaging pathways, than in pathways with mailed or home-based tests.
- **H6:** Exploratory: the digital-exclusion gradient is larger in countries or subcontexts with more opportunistic and digitally complex screening pathways than in highly organized call-recall systems. citeturn13view6turn16search1turn38view2turn24view1
